# Personas

Four personas drive every product decision in VoiceIQ. Each has a distinct job to be done, and the product's information architecture (list view → theme view → opportunity view → executive view) maps directly onto them, from most raw to most synthesized.

---

## Priya Nair — Product Manager

**Role:** Senior PM at a B2B SaaS company, owns a product area with ~15k active accounts.

**Job to be done:** Decide what to build next, and defend that decision with evidence rather than "the loudest customer asked for it."

**Current workflow (without VoiceIQ):** Skims a Zendesk view, a spreadsheet of sales-call notes, and whatever came up in the last exec sync. Prioritization is largely vibes-based, backed by whichever anecdote she remembers most vividly.

**What she needs from VoiceIQ:**
- A ranked opportunity list, not a raw feedback firehose
- Confidence that a theme represents dozens or hundreds of customers, not one loud account
- A defensible score she can put in a roadmap review deck

**Success looks like:** She opens VoiceIQ before a roadmap planning meeting and leaves with her top 3 bets already ranked and evidenced.

---

## Devon Chu — UX Researcher

**Role:** Runs qualitative research for the product org; often the person asked to "go find out why."

**Job to be done:** Trace a theme back to verbatim customer language and connect it to who is actually saying it.

**Current workflow (without VoiceIQ):** Manually re-reads raw tickets and call notes any time someone asks "what's the actual customer language behind this request?" Personas are built once, from a handful of interviews, and rarely revisited.

**What she needs from VoiceIQ:**
- One click from a theme down to the original verbatim feedback items behind it
- Personas that are derived from actual clustered data, not a single round of interviews frozen in time
- Enough traceability to defend a finding when someone pushes back on it

**Success looks like:** When a stakeholder asks "who exactly wants this," she has an immediate, evidence-backed answer instead of a research project.

---

## Marcus Webb — Customer Success / Support Lead

**Role:** Manages a support team; feedback logging has to fit inside an already-full day.

**Job to be done:** Make sure what customers tell his team doesn't die in a closed ticket. Get it in front of product with minimal extra effort.

**Current workflow (without VoiceIQ):** Occasionally forwards a particularly compelling ticket to a PM directly. Most feedback signal is trapped in ticket text that nobody revisits in aggregate.

**What he needs from VoiceIQ:**
- Logging or tagging feedback in seconds, not minutes — friction here means his team stops doing it
- Confidence that flagging something actually surfaces it, so the habit feels worth keeping
- No need to understand clustering, scoring, or any of the underlying mechanics

**Success looks like:** Tagging feedback becomes as automatic as closing a ticket, because he can see it actually influences the roadmap.

---

## Elena Vasquez — Executive (VP Product / CPO)

**Role:** Owns the product org's roadmap and has to justify it upward to the CEO and board.

**Job to be done:** Understand, at a glance and without reading raw tickets, what customers are asking for this month and why it matters to revenue and churn.

**Current workflow (without VoiceIQ):** Relies on PMs to synthesize and present; the synthesis quality varies by presenter, and there's no consistent, org-wide view of customer sentiment trends.

**What she needs from VoiceIQ:**
- A top-line narrative view, not a dashboard she has to interpret herself
- Themes connected to business impact (revenue at risk, churn signal), not just volume
- Enough consistency that she can compare this month to last month

**Success looks like:** She pulls up VoiceIQ's executive summary before a board meeting and it already says, in plain language, what's shifting and why it matters.
